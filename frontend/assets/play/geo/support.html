Support

Applies to: Geo — the Android app (with a Wear OS companion) published by nettrash on Google Play (me.nettrash.geo).

Need help?

Email nettrash@nettrash.me. Replies usually arrive within two working days. Please include your phone model, your Android version, and the app version (shown on the Info tab's data-source / about section) — it makes sensor and AR issues much easier to reproduce.

Geo is a hiking instrument: live altitude, atmospheric pressure, a storm-warning barometer, an offline-capable augmented-reality view that names the mountains around you, plus a trip recorder and a summit log. This page covers the most common questions; if you don't find your answer, the email link above reaches a human.

Frequently asked questions

Why is the altitude reading 0 (or missing)?

Geo reads altitude from the phone's barometric pressure sensor. Not every Android phone has one — if yours doesn't, barometric altitude can't be shown (the GPS altitude is still used where available once Location is granted). High-rate sensor access is also needed for the AR view to keep peak labels steady; that's the HIGH_SAMPLING_RATE_SENSORS permission, which is normal/install-time on Android.

The altitude is off by tens of metres. Can I fix it?

Yes — barometric altitude drifts with the weather, so Geo offers known-elevation calibration. At a point with a known height (a trailhead sign, a summit marker, a benchmark), tap the barometer card on the Info tab and enter "I am at X m". Geo pins the altimeter to that value instantly — no network needed — and shows a green calibrated badge. The pin gently decays over about six hours toward the live reference pressure and then reverts, so a stale calibration can't silently bias your altitude. The calibrated value is shared with the home-screen widget and the background worker so they all agree.

What is the "pressure falling fast" notification?

That's the storm warning — the classic mountaineering/sailing use of a barometer. Geo watches the 3-hour pressure tendency (corrected for any altitude you've gained, so a climb doesn't false-trigger it) and posts a single local notification, on a dedicated Weather alerts channel, when pressure drops sharply. It alerts at most once per onset. To receive it, allow notifications when Geo first asks, or enable them later in Settings → Apps → Geo → Notifications; denying them just turns the advisory off. A live 3-hour trend chip also appears on the Info barometer card. Because Android throttles background work, treat the alert as best-effort.

Why are no peaks shown in the AR view?

Peaks are looked up from OpenStreetMap, which needs network access, and the Nature (AR) view needs ARCore plus a clear line of sight to the sky. Pointing the camera at an interior wall shows the camera feed and a "Scanning…" indicator but no peak labels — point it out a window or step outside. If your area genuinely has no catalogued peaks within the 5 km search radius, the view shows the horizon line by itself. Heading somewhere with no signal? See offline expedition packs below.

How do offline expedition packs work?

Before you lose signal, pre-cache an area so the Nature (AR) view keeps naming peaks and drawing the terrain skyline on a no-signal summit. From the Info tab → "Manage offline areas" (or the Map tab, using "Download a region" to frame an area on the map), download the area around a location at a 5 / 10 / 50 / 100 km radius. Geo fetches that area's named peaks and the terrain-elevation grid and stores them on the device; offline, those peaks and the skyline resolve from the cache with no live calls. Saved packs list their peak/cell counts and date, can be named and renamed, and are shown as circles on the Map tab. Map tiles aren't cached (Google Maps licensing), so the basemap still needs a connection — only the AR peaks and skyline work fully offline. Delete a pack any time from the same screen.

Why is the horizon line a different colour / flat?

The terrain skyline is drawn from real elevation data fetched from the Open-Meteo elevation API. When that service is briefly unreachable and you don't have an offline pack covering the area, Geo falls back to a geometric sea-level horizon. Both track your camera correctly; the difference is how realistic the line is over distant mountains. (Geo 1.1 switched the elevation source to Open-Meteo, which is much faster and more reliable than the previous provider.)

How do I identify a peak in the AR view, or share the view?

Tap any peak label or marker to open its detail sheet — name, altitude, distance, bearing, coordinates, and a Directions button. Named peaks that form the skyline silhouette have their name and elevation welded right onto the ridge line, joined by a thin leader line to the exact point on the horizon. To share what you're looking at, tap the shutter button: Geo freezes the current camera frame with the peak labels and skyline composited on top and shares it through the Android share sheet. The capture is created entirely on-device and only leaves the app if you pick a destination.

What is the summit log, and how does the auto-detect work?

When you walk within roughly 500 m of a known peak (a Seven Summit, a Snow Leopard peak, or other catalogued summit), Geo offers to log the ascent — it never logs automatically; you confirm. A logged summit records the date, the peak's elevation, your measured barometric altitude, and an optional note, and appears in the "Summits" trophy case on the Stat tab, each with a detail view (editable note, Directions, Delete, and a shareable summit card). Everything is stored on-device. Only the peak's public location is stored or shared — never a record of where you actually were.

How do I record a trip?

On the Stat tab, tap to start the trip recorder; it wraps Geo's always-on barometer/GPS sampling into a named outing. Each trip shows total ascent and descent (with sub-3 m sensor noise smoothed out so the numbers don't inflate), max/min altitude, distance, moving time, and an elevation profile. Recording survives an app restart and runs entirely on-device. Stop the trip from the same control when you're done.

Where is my history stored, and how do I delete it?

Entirely on your device, in a local database — your altitude/pressure history, recorded trips, and summit log are not synced anywhere and are not backed up to your Google Drive. To delete it: use Clear history on the Stat tab (and delete individual trips or summits from their detail views), or uninstall Geo / clear its storage in Settings → Apps → Geo → Storage. History older than about a year is also pruned automatically. Full detail is in the privacy policy.

Which way is the peak? The compass arrow.

The closest- and highest-mountain cards on the Info tab show the true bearing to the peak ("117° SE") with an arrow that rotates to your live heading, corrected to true north via the local magnetic declination, so it always points at the summit — a low-power, AR-free way to find a peak. If the arrow wanders, you'll see a "calibrate compass" hint; move the phone in a figure-eight to settle the magnetometer. The compass runs only while the Info tab is open.

The home-screen widget shows stale or zero data.

Android refreshes widgets on its own schedule, and Geo also pushes a fresh snapshot whenever it captures a new barometer reading. If you've just installed the app, open it once and give the widget a minute to catch up. A widget showing a "no data" / staleness cue (rather than an authoritative 0 m) means it hasn't received a reading yet — open the app to seed one. You can reconfigure the widget by long-pressing it; your toggle choices are preserved.

My Wear OS tile / data isn't updating.

The watch reads its own barometer and exchanges history with the phone over the Wearable Data Layer when the two are paired and reachable. Open the Geo app on the watch once and let it run for a few seconds; calibration and barometer state are persisted and restored across relaunches, and watch-captured readings backfill to the phone's history when they reconnect.

Troubleshooting

The AR camera view is black or won't start.

The Nature (AR) view needs camera permission and Google Play Services for AR (ARCore), which not every device supports. Grant camera access in Settings → Apps → Geo → Permissions → Camera, make sure ARCore is installed/updated from the Play Store, and give the scene a second to lock tracking — Geo shows just the camera + crosshair until tracking is ready.

The AR overlay points the wrong way.

The overlay is welded to true north using your compass heading and the local magnetic declination. If it looks rotated, move the phone in a figure-eight to recalibrate the magnetometer, away from metal and magnets. (Geo 1.1 fixed a true-north alignment issue in the AR scene.)

"Directions" did nothing.

Directions open a maps app via a generic geo: location intent. If no maps app is installed to handle it, nothing opens — install Google Maps (or any maps app) and try again.

The map is blank or won't render tiles.

The Map tab needs a network connection (map tiles are never cached for offline use, by Google Maps licensing). Offline expedition packs cover the AR peaks and skyline, not the basemap. If the map is blank even online, check that the device has a working connection and try reopening the tab.

Privacy

Geo holds no account and no data about you. Everything you record stays on your device; the only network calls are to render maps, look up nearby peaks (OpenStreetMap), and fetch terrain elevation and reference pressure (Open-Meteo). There are no analytics, advertising, or crash-reporting SDKs. Full statement: Privacy Policy.

Reporting bugs

If something looks wrong, please describe what you did, what you expected, and what actually happened. A screenshot or screen recording helps a lot. Send it to nettrash@nettrash.me; we'll respond as soon as we can.
