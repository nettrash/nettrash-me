Privacy Policy

Effective date: 16 May 2026 · Applies to: Exchange — the Android app published by nettrash on Google Play (me.nettrash.exchange), and the same upload-key-signed APK side-loaded from nettrash.me/play/exchange/exchange-latest.apk.

This policy is versioned alongside the app's source code; the most recent commit on main is authoritative.

TL;DR

Exchange does not collect, transmit, sell, or share any personal data. It contains no analytics, no advertising SDKs, no third-party trackers, and no remote servers operated by us. Your private keys, your recipients, and every message you encrypt or decrypt stay on your device.

If that already answers your question, you don't need to read the rest.

What Exchange does

Exchange is an end-to-end encryption tool. You type a message, pick a recipient whose public key you have on file, and the app hands back a single base64 line — the EXC2: envelope. You paste that line into whichever messenger you already use (Signal, Telegram, WhatsApp, SMS, e-mail, anything that carries text). Only the recipient can read it; the signature proves it came from you. Decryption works the same way in reverse: paste an envelope you received, see the plaintext.

Exchange does not send messages itself. The carrier app you paste into is the one that handles delivery; Exchange is never in the loop.

What we collect

Nothing. Exchange does not transmit any data about you to us or to any third party. There is no account to create, no email to register, and no telemetry pinging home from the app.

The app does not even request the android.permission.INTERNET permission. It cannot open a socket. Every byte that leaves the app does so because you, the user, told a different app (your messenger, mail client, browser) to carry it.

The cryptographic primitives Exchange uses (Curve25519 X25519 / Ed25519, ChaCha20-Poly1305, HKDF-SHA256, PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA256) are provided by the bundled BouncyCastle library and run entirely on-device. Random bytes come from the Android system SecureRandom. QR-code generation and decoding use ZXing, also on-device.

Data stored on your device

Exchange keeps two pieces of state, both strictly local to your device:

  • Your identity — a 64-byte private-key blob (32 bytes of X25519 + 32 bytes of Ed25519). It is wrapped under an AES-256/GCM key generated inside the Android Keystore, which on supported hardware sits inside the TEE (Trusted Execution Environment) or StrongBox. The wrapped ciphertext lives in the app's private exchange.identity.v1 SharedPreferences file. Even a forensic dump of the device's user partition yields ciphertext only — without arbitrary code execution on the device, the underlying keys cannot be lifted.
  • Your recipient list — a local SQLite database managed by Android's Room library at /data/data/me.nettrash.exchange/databases/exchange.db. Each row contains the display name you assigned, the contact's two public keys, an 8-byte fingerprint derived from those keys, your free-form notes, and a creation timestamp. On a non-rooted device, no other app can read it.

Auto Backup is disabled (android:allowBackup="false") and the data_extraction_rules.xml file additionally excludes every storage domain (files, databases, shared-preferences, external storage, root). The identity and recipient database cannot leave the device via any Google-managed backup, device-transfer, or restore channel.

If you want to move your identity to another device, Exchange offers two explicit, end-to-end-encrypted paths — see Backup & transfer below.

Deleting the app, or clearing its storage from Settings → Apps → Exchange → Storage, removes everything Exchange has ever written: the identity blob, the Keystore wrapping key, and the recipient database.

Backup & transfer

Both flows below produce ciphertext that you, and only you, can decrypt. Neither involves any server, and neither leaves the device automatically.

  • Passphrase backup (EXCBKP1:<base64>). Your identity and your recipient list are JSON-serialised, AEAD-encrypted with ChaCha20-Poly1305 under a key derived from your passphrase via 600,000 iterations of PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA256, and emitted as one base64 line. Save the line wherever you keep your other secrets (a password manager works well); paste it back into Settings → Import identity on a future device. There is no way for us — or anyone else — to decrypt the blob without your passphrase.
  • In-person identity QR (EXCQR1:<base64>). For two devices you physically hold at the same time, Exchange can render the identity blob as a QR code encrypted under a fresh 32-byte symmetric key that is embedded in the QR itself. The receiving phone's camera reads it once, decrypts, and overwrites its local identity after you confirm the fingerprint matches. The QR is the secret; keep it visible only to the second device.

Permissions Exchange asks for, and what each one is used for

Permission Used for Scope
Camera (android.permission.CAMERA) Scanning QR codes — either a contact's public key bundle (when adding a recipient) or an identity transfer QR (when receiving an identity from your other device). Frames are decoded on-device by ZXing. Nothing is recorded; no image is written to disk; nothing is transmitted.

Several familiar permissions are not requested:

  • Internet (android.permission.INTERNET) — not declared. The app cannot open a socket. Every smart action that opens a URL is delegated to the system, which launches your browser; the network request is performed by the browser, not by Exchange.
  • READ_CONTACTS / WRITE_CONTACTS — Exchange does not read or write your address book. The recipient list is internal to the app.
  • READ_MEDIA_IMAGES / READ_EXTERNAL_STORAGE — Exchange does not import pictures or scan QRs from your photo library; QR codes are read live through the camera.
  • ACCESS_FINE_LOCATION / ACCESS_COARSE_LOCATION — never requested. Exchange has no use for your location.

Share-to-Exchange and deep links

Beyond pasting into the Decrypt screen by hand, Exchange can receive envelopes through Android's normal sharing surfaces:

  • Share Sheet (ACTION_SEND with text/plain). When you long-press an EXC2: line in any messenger and choose Share → Exchange, the system passes the text to Exchange. The text is held in memory, decrypted with your identity, and shown on the Decrypt screen. Nothing is persisted unless you copy the plaintext out.
  • App Links (https://exchange.nettrash.me/msg?p=… and the exc2://… custom scheme). Tap such a link and Android opens Exchange directly with the envelope pre-loaded for decryption. The system intent verifier confirms the link belongs to Exchange by fetching https://exchange.nettrash.me/.well-known/assetlinks.json on install — that fetch is performed by Play Services, not by Exchange.

Third-party services

None operating off the device. Exchange ships with the BouncyCastle cryptographic library, the ZXing QR library, the Jetpack libraries (Compose, Material 3, Room, CameraX, Navigation, DataStore), and the Google Android Material Components library — solely for the splash-window theme. None of them contact the network at runtime in this app. There are no analytics tools (Firebase, Mixpanel, Sentry, etc.), no advertising SDKs (AdMob, Meta, AppLovin, etc.), no crash reporters that phone home, and no attribution providers.

Specifically, the Google Advertising ID is not requested, accessed, or used. Exchange declares this on its Google Play Data Safety form.

Tracking

Exchange does not "track" you in any sense Google Play recognises in its Data Safety taxonomy: it does not link any data collected in the app with data from other apps, websites, or offline sources to build a user profile, and it does not share any data with data brokers. Exchange is therefore declared with Data not collected and Data not shared on its Google Play Data Safety form.

Children's privacy

Exchange is rated for general audiences (Everyone / PEGI 3) and is suitable for all ages. We do not knowingly collect personal information from children, because we do not collect personal information from anyone. Exchange is not a member of Google's Designed for Families programme — the app is a general-purpose utility, not directed at children under 13.

International data transfers

Because Exchange does not transmit personal data anywhere, there are no cross-border transfers to disclose under the GDPR, UK GDPR, CCPA, LGPD, or similar regimes.

Your rights

Because we hold no data about you:

  • There is no record to access under GDPR Article 15 / CCPA "right to know".
  • There is no record to delete under GDPR Article 17 / CCPA "right to delete" (the local identity and recipient database are yours alone — clearing the app's storage or uninstalling the app removes them entirely).
  • There is no record to correct under GDPR Article 16.
  • There is nothing being sold or shared under CCPA / CPRA, so no opt-out is required.

If you'd like confirmation in writing that we hold no data about you, email the address below and we will reply.

Cryptographic protocol

The on-the-wire envelope format (EXC2), the passphrase backup format (EXCBKP1), and the identity-transfer QR format (EXCQR1) are fully specified and open. They are byte-compatible with the iOS Exchange app of the same name. The reference implementation and its property-based test suite are published under the MIT licence at github.com/nettrash/exchange-android and github.com/nettrash/exchange-ios.
