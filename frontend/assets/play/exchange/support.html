Support

Applies to: Exchange — the Android app published by nettrash on Google Play (me.nettrash.exchange).

Need help?

Email nettrash@nettrash.me. Replies usually arrive within two working days. Please include your Android version and the app version (shown in Settings → About → Version).

What Exchange is

Exchange is an end-to-end encryption tool you wrap around whichever messenger you already use. You type a message inside Exchange, pick a recipient whose public key you already have on file, and Exchange hands back a single line starting with EXC2:. Paste that line into Signal, Telegram, WhatsApp, SMS, e-mail — anything that carries text. Only your recipient can decrypt it; only your signing key proves it came from you.

Exchange itself does not send messages and does not connect to any server. The carrier app you paste into is the one that delivers the bytes.

Quick start

  1. Install Exchange on both devices that want to communicate.
  2. On each device, tap the gear icon to find the public-key bundle, or use Show QR code from the home screen.
  3. On the other device, tap the + button (Add recipient), give the person a name, then either paste their bundle or tap Scan QR to read it from their screen. Repeat in the opposite direction so each device knows the other.
  4. Verify the fingerprint. Both phones show an 8-byte fingerprint in groups of four hex characters. Read it out loud and compare. If the fingerprints match, the key was not intercepted in transit.
  5. Tap the pencil icon to Compose. Pick a recipient, type a message, tap Encrypt & sign. Copy the resulting line or hit Share to hand it to your messenger.
  6. To decrypt: tap Decrypt, paste the line you received (or tap an https://exchange.nettrash.me/msg link from any app), and read the plaintext.

Common tasks

Add a recipient

From the home screen, tap the + in the top bar. You can either paste a base64 public-key bundle the other person sent you, or tap Scan QR to read it from their My identity screen. Give the recipient a display name. Compare the fingerprint Exchange computes against what they see on their device. If they match, tap Save.

Share your own public key

Tap the home-screen card Show QR code. Hand the phone to the other person to scan, or tap Copy public key bundle to share it through any channel you trust (the bundle is base64 and safe to send in cleartext).

Back up your identity (passphrase)

Settings → Export identity (passphrase). Pick a passphrase you will not forget; Exchange enforces a minimum length of 12 characters. The exported blob is one base64 line starting with EXCBKP1: — store it in a password manager.

On the new device: Settings → Import identity (passphrase). Paste the blob, enter the same passphrase, confirm the fingerprint matches the one on your old device, then tap Replace local identity. Your recipient list comes across with it.

Move to a new device (in-person QR)

When you hold both phones at the same time:

  1. On the old device, open Settings → Send identity (QR code).
  2. On the new device, open Settings → Receive identity (Scan QR).
  3. Point the new device's camera at the old device's screen.
  4. The new device shows the decoded fingerprint. Confirm it matches what the old device shows, then tap Replace local identity.

The QR contains your private key encrypted with a key embedded in the same QR. Anyone who photographs the QR can decrypt the identity, so keep it visible to your second device only.

Receive an encrypted message from any messenger

Three paths, pick whichever is most convenient:

  • Tap the link. If the message was sent as a https://exchange.nettrash.me/msg?p=… URL, just tap it. Android opens Exchange and the plaintext appears.
  • Share into Exchange. Long-press the EXC2: line in your messenger, choose Share → Exchange. The plaintext appears.
  • Copy & paste. Copy the line, open Exchange, tap Decrypt, then Paste from clipboard. Exchange normalises both raw EXC2: envelopes and URL forms.

Reset your identity

Settings → Reset identity wipes your private key and every saved recipient. A new identity is generated on the next launch. Anything previously encrypted to your old key becomes unreadable. This cannot be undone — make sure you have a backup if there is content you still need to decrypt.

Troubleshooting

"This message wasn't sent to your identity"

The envelope's recipient fingerprint doesn't match yours. Most likely the sender encrypted to a different identity of yours (e.g. an old key on a phone you've replaced without importing the backup), or the message is genuinely addressed to someone else. If you have an EXCBKP1 backup of the older identity, import it on a spare device and decrypt there.

"The sender's signature didn't verify"

The envelope was modified after it was signed. The modification can be deliberate (someone tampered with the payload) or accidental (the messenger or pasteboard stripped characters). Ask the sender to re-share, ideally as the https://exchange.nettrash.me/msg/… URL form, which survives more aggressive auto-formatting.

"The message is corrupt or has been tampered with"

The signature was valid but the inner AEAD decryption failed. Usually a transport glitch — try having the sender re-share the envelope.

"The passphrase didn't decrypt the backup"

Either the passphrase is wrong (check Caps Lock and any leading/trailing spaces from copy-paste) or the EXCBKP1 blob was truncated. PBKDF2 takes about a second on a modern phone, so wait for the Decrypt spinner to finish before retrying.

"This doesn't look like an Exchange envelope"

The pasted text didn't start with EXC2: and didn't match the URL form either. Re-copy the envelope — some apps add surrounding whitespace or quote marks that break the prefix detection. If Exchange detects a partial URL or a code-fenced block, scrub it down to just the EXC2:… line.

QR scanner refuses to open

Exchange needs camera permission to scan QR codes. If you previously denied it, open Settings → Apps → Exchange → Permissions → Camera and switch it to Allow only while using the app, then re-open the scanner.

"A recipient with this key already exists"

You're adding a public key Exchange already has on file under a different name. Delete the old entry from the home screen first if you want to rename, or leave it in place — duplicate fingerprints are blocked deliberately so you can't accidentally fork the same contact.

Privacy & security

Exchange holds no data about you. It does not request the INTERNET permission and cannot open a socket; it does not have analytics, advertising, or crash-reporting SDKs. Your identity lives in the Android Keystore (hardware backed where the device supports it), and your recipient list is a local SQLite database that no other app can read. Full statement: Privacy Policy.

Cross-platform

Exchange envelopes round-trip between the Android app and the iOS app of the same name. The wire format is fully specified and stable across both. You can move your identity from Android to iPhone (and back) using the EXCBKP1 passphrase backup, but not the QR transfer — the QR flow currently requires both devices to be on the same platform because the camera-based wire format expects a freshly generated symmetric key with no cross-platform Cocoa entitlement check.

Open source

Exchange is released under the MIT licence. Source code, issue tracker, and the property-based crypto test suite live at github.com/nettrash/exchange-android for Android and github.com/nettrash/exchange-ios for iOS.

Please file bug reports and feature requests as GitHub issues — they are public by design so other users can see what's already known. For private security disclosures or questions about your personal data, email nettrash@nettrash.me directly.
